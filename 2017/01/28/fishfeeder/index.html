Wifi Enabled Automatic Fish Feeder

Meet Ballooney.

He's a fish. He likes to eat (go figure). He's our family pet that is lowest maintenance of them all. Second up is our dog Frankie, who cant be trusted (more on that in a later post). This post documents my journey to find an automatic fish feeder. On occasion we take family vacations, and this year will mark the first year we've taken a vacation since getting Ballooney. What to do with the fish? We could take him with us. Somehow though, lugging a fish in a bag for four hours in a car and setting him up when we get there, seems less like a vacation for us, and more for him. Perhaps, maybe, we could find an auto feeder for him? To the interwebs!

What can we buy?

Feeder 1

This feeder initially looked promising from the design point of view (assuming it worked). Looking at the reviews on amazon, I see a nice inverted trend with 1 star in the lead at 30%. This makes me wonder about build quality. Anyone want to play the $15 lottery? Also there is likely no control of the amount of food dispensed because of the way the mechanism is designed.

Feeder 2

Now this one looked promising too. It even had a stable looking mount. The catch is its not a beta fish feeder. Ballooney eats these tiny pellet things that I could not even imagine trying to meter out of the sliding "adjustment" door illustrated in the add video.

Gut Feel

As far as I can tell, having the mechanism inside the tank it a bad idea and is a no go in my mind. I looked around for a while and could not find a feeder that I thought would result in:

  1. Ballooney being alive when we return
  2. House intact from not burning down when feeder falls into pool of water. (Over cautious I know)

Besides this gives me an excuse to play with toys! Which is far more fun than just buying something off the shelf.

Lets Just Build One: Design Time!

Before starting any engineering project we first have to ask ourselves what exactly is it we need? In this case we have a defined set of requirements.

  • Reliably provide a fixed amount of food
  • Solid mount that is not based on suction cups
  • A desire for positive feedback (did it actually feed the fish)
  • Fault tolerant

A few years ago on a pure impulse buy, I purchased a handful of ESP8266 boards. These are great little Wi-Fi boards at only $10. This solves quite of few of the requirements. Having an Internet connection is a boon for this project because we can use the network time protocol (NTP) to give the ESP8266 the current time. The on board flash means we can store the current state of affairs. This means that if all goes well we can recover gracefully after a power failure.

The firmware contains integrated slack notifications. On startup the firmware will send a notification to your slack account with current NTP time as well as the current feeder position. Additionally there will be a notification when the fish is fed. (Currently set to 8AM/8PM EST).

Below are links to the STL cad models I used to print the housing. The source code for the ESP-8266 is also in the same github repo.

Feeder Wheel

The main "feeding" component of this design is a wheel mounted above a flange. Each hole in the wheel will hold around four pellets of food. The bottom flange has a single hole over the position over the water. Basic theory of operation is that a servo motor will drive the top wheel forward, line up the top hole positions over the bottom hole, and release the food.

Flange

This is the underlying flange mounted below the feeder wheel.

Hanger

This hanger is sized to hold the ESP-8266 module, and small HS-55 servo.

Back Cover

This box dove tails into the hanger above giving a polished look to the project. There is a small gap around the flange, but hey, good enough for a weekend of work.

Assembled View

This is what the design looks like assembled.

Conclusion

Below is what the feeder looks like installed. The button you see is used to reset the feeder back to the home position. I'd press this to ensure a clean reset when leaving unattended for a period of time. I can report that the feeder worked marvelously for the weeks leading up to our trip. Once on the trip however, there was accident. From what we can tell, Ballooney attacked the rocks and the rocks attacked back. This resulted in his demise under a pile of rocks and correctly timed food drops. While he did not survive, he was well fed and I continued to get notifications that he was fed.
